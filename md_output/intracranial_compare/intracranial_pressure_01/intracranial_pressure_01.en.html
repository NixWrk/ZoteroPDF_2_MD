A Novel Intracranial Pressure Readout Circuit for Passive Wireless LC Sensor

Fa Wang , Member, IEEE , Xuan Zhang, Mehdi Shokoueinejad , Member, IEEE , Bermans J. Iskandar, Joshua E. Medow, and John G. Webster , Life Fellow, IEEE

Abstract —We present a wide frequency range, low cost, wireless intracranial pressure monitoring system, which includes an implantable passive sensor and an external reader. The passive sensor consists of two spiral coils and transduces the pressure change to a resonant frequency shift. The external portable reader reads out the sensor's resonant frequency over a wide frequency range (35 MHz–2.7 GHz). We propose a novel circuit topology, which tracks the system's impedance and phase change at a high frequency with low-cost components. This circuit is very simple and reliable. A prototype has been developed, and measurement results demonstrate that the device achieves a suitable measurement distance ( > 2 cm), sufficient sample frequency ( > 6 Hz), fine resolution, and good measurement accuracy for medical practice. Responsivity of this prototype is 0.92 MHz/mmHg and resolution is 0.028 mmHg. COMSOL specific absorption rate simulation proves that this system is safe. Considerations to improve the device performance have been discussed, which include the size of antenna, the power radiation, the Analog-to-digital converter (ADC) choice, and the signal processing algorithm.

Index Terms —Biomedical measurements, intracranial pressure, passive pressure telemetry, readout circuit.

I. INTRODUCTION

I NCREASED pressure within the brain from fluid accumulation, known as hydrocephalus, affects one to two in every 1000 live births, making it one of the most common birth defects, more common than Down syndrome or deafness. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) website estimates 700,000 children and adults living with hydrocephalus, and it is by far the leading cause of brain surgery for children in the US.

Use of a catheter to link the intracranial pressure (ICP) sensor across the skin increases the risk of complications, such as infection, traumatic hemorrhage, and malfunction of the device [1], motivating our interests to wirelessly measure ICP.

Telemetric monitors used for ICP monitoring was firstly described in 1990s [2]. Then fully implantable MEMs-based/ Microtelemetry ICP sensor, portable read out monitor and wireless communication method to read out data were developed [3]–[7]. A wireless ICP sensor could be passive or active. An active sensor combines the pressure sensor and a small on-chip communication module like Bluetooth [8], [9]. The active sensor allows a longer communication distance between the sensor and external reader, and could achieve better signal to noise ratio (SNR). However, an active sensor needs a power supply, which leads to larger size, larger weight, and periodic battery replacement. Thus an active sensor prohibits long term continuous ICP monitoring. Our passive resonant sensor is based on a tank circuit. Pressure can be measured using the sensor by measuring the resonant frequency of the tank. Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS) technology has helped build the ultrasmall passive resonant sensors. The device could be easily implanted into the human brain, with minimal disturbance to nearby tissues. Chen et al . reported a flexible passive pressure sensor resonant at 2 GHz with dimensions of 1 × 1 × 0.1 mm [6]. Other studies reported ultrasmall sensors ranging from 350 MHz [10] to 2.4 GHz [5], [11]. These passive sensors measured ICP using an impedance analyzer [12]–[14] or Vector Network Analyzer (VNA) [3], [5], [6], which is heavy, expensive, nonportable, and requires a separate power supply. In addition, these measurements could only be done in a laboratory manually, which prohibits continuous daily ICP monitoring.

Previous studies [15]–[17] developed similar telemetry devices based on traditional grid dip oscillator technology. But grid dip oscillator technology has some constraints which prevent it from being applied to ultrasmall sensors, which always resonate at GHz frequencies: First, its sweeping frequency range is limited by the tuner capacitor, thus the frequency range is usually around 20 MHz (10 MHz to 30 MHz in [15]; 22–41 MHz [16]; 12.76 and 13.01 MHz in [17]), which is narrow. Second, the center and highest frequency of the oscillator is limited by the oscillator's parasitic capacitance, which won't reach up to the GHz range. Third, the oscillator's resonant frequency is determined by the imaginary portion of the system, including the antenna and the sensor. Especially when the sweeping frequency approaches the sensor resonant frequency, the capacitance components of the sensor influence the oscillator's resonant frequency to form a "frequency drift". This phenomenon inhibits us from getting accurate measurement results when trying to detect the tiny pressure change of the sensor. Also, this is an analog

In addition, the output frequency is only determined by the microcontroller's commands, and is isolated from the antenna and sensor.

Measurement distance is also a challenge and a significant consideration for ICP monitor design. Nopper et al. proposed a reader that could achieve a measurement distance of 8.5 mm [15]. Coosemans et al. proposed a similar reader for intraocular pressure measurement with maximum distance of 7.5 mm [16]. In real practice, the male adult scalp thickness ranges from 2 to 3.5 mm [18], and skull thickness average ranges from 6.08 mm to 8.09 mm, differs with location, and standard deviations range from 1.15 to 1.88 mm [19]. The dura is a thick membrane surrounding the brain, which has an average thickness of 0.27 mm [20]. So the minimal measurement distance should be at least 10 mm and desirably larger than 15 mm.

II. METHODOLOGY

A. System Model

A passive pressure sensor, which is implanted under the dura mater, consists of two spiral inductors. Fig. 1 shows that increasing ICP bends the plates and increases the capacitance between the two inductors. These components form a resonant tank, and the self-resonant frequency shifts corresponding to the ICP change. Fig. 2 shows that an external reader placed outside the head utilizes an antenna to inductively couple with the sensor, and excites a sweep frequency signal to the sensor. The reader is light (48 g excluding the package box) and small size (72 \(\times\) 48 \(\times\) 20 mm) that could be built into a helmet for patients' continuous ICP monitoring and could be handheld for hourly or daily measurement. Based on our measurement result, this pair of resistor and capacitor works well in the range of 400 MHz to 1 GHz. III. PROTOTYPE

A. Device Overview

Fig. 8 shows the device flow chart. An RF signal generator is controlled by a microcontroller to sweep in the frequency range

Fig. 8. Flow chart of system design.

in which the sensor's resonant frequency located. An RF amplifier enhances the RF signal with a constant power output when the load impedance matched (9 dBm when load equals 50 ). When the antenna-sensor coupling system forms the load, any change of the antenna-sensor coupling system's impedance results in the change of the RF amplifier output. It is hard to measure the RF amplifier output directly due to its high frequency. A half-wave rectifier recovers the envelope amplitude. The amplitude value is sent back to the microcontroller for signal processing. At the same time, it is also sent to a NI DAQ, which could help us to achieve real time monitoring on the laptop. After each sweep ended, it is recorded by LabVIEW as a txt file. Fig. 8 shows MATLAB software processes the signal data.

B. RF Signal Generator and Microcontroller

Currently, diaphragm based pressure sensors for ICP or intraocular pressure (IOP) measurement resonate from 63 MHz to 2.45 GHz [5], [6], [21], [22]. A slight difference of coil pattern design on the chip or distance between coils results in a huge difference in resonant frequency. Thus this telemetry device is adapted to an ultra-wide frequency range.

An ADF4351 chip (Analog Devices) is the main component for the RF signal generator. The ADF4351 has an integrated voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO) with an output frequency ranging from 2200 MHz to 4400 MHz. In addition, divide-by-1/-2/-4/-8/-16/-32/-64 circuits allow the user to generate RF output frequencies as low as 35 MHz. The output frequency is set by six inner registers through an SPI interface. A Microcontroller dsPIC33FJ32GP202 writes these registers to make the ADF4351 generate a sweeping RF signal in a specified range. The dsPIC33FJ32GP202 chip is a low cost microcontroller with an advanced built in ADC. This ADC measures the amplitude of the envelope of the RF amplifier output signal.

D. Measurement Resolution and Measurement Accuracy

The frequency-to-pressure responsivity of this sample PCB sensor is 0.92 MHz/mmHg. The MEMS sensor usually has lower responsivity because of the high Young's modulus of silicon, for example: 120 kHz/mmHg [12]; 160 kHz/mmHg [10]. As shown in Fig. 15, the bandwidth of telemetry device at 600 MHz is 24 kHz. To achieve maximal resolution, we could design the sweep step as 24 kHz (minimal step length form ADF4351 is 0 kHz). As shown in Fig. 13 for our current PCB sensor, average frequency drop is around 11.75 MHz for every 10 mmHg pressure increase. Based on this limitation, the minimal resolution could be 0.028 mmHg.

V. DISCUSSION

The section discusses the considerations which may improve the measurement distance of the telemetry device.

A. Antenna Radius

Fig. 5 shows maximal amplitude of signal drop at resonant frequency, is positively correlated to \(k^2Q\) . Antenna geometry influences the k factor, which was implemented in the design.

The mutual inductance between the telemetry device antenna and spiral coil on the sensor is specified by

\[k = \frac{L_m}{\sqrt{L_1 L_2}} \tag{4}\]

\(L_{\rm m}\) is calculated as [23]:

\[L_{\rm m} = \left(\frac{4}{\pi}\right)^2 \sum_{i=1}^{i=n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{j=n_b} M_{ij}\] (5)

\[M_{ij} = \frac{\mu_0 \pi a_i^2 b_j^2}{2(a_i^2 + b_i^2 + z^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}} \left( 1 + \frac{15}{32} \gamma_{ij}^2 + \frac{315}{1024} \gamma_{ij}^4 \right)\] (6)

\[\gamma_{ij=\frac{2a_ib_j}{a_i^2+b_j^2}}\tag{7}\]

where \(\mu\) is the vacuum magnetic permeability: \(\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\) . \(a_i\) is the i th turn radius of antenna, and \(b_j\) is the j th turn radius of the sensor's coil, z is the distance between antenna and sensor.

Planar spiral inductor's inductance has been well studied. Mohan et al. described that, for the square shape planar spiral inductor, the inductance is expressed as [24]

\[L = \frac{2\mu_0}{\pi} n^2 d_{avg} \left( \ln \left( \frac{2.07}{\varphi} \right) + 0.18\varphi + 0.13\varphi^2 \right)\] (8)

\(d_{avg}\) is the average diameter of the square, which is \(\frac{D+D_{in}}{2}\) . \(\varphi = \frac{1-\alpha}{1+\alpha}\) , \(\alpha\) is the ratio of outer diameter to inner diameter.

B. More Radiation Power and RF Safety

The maximum power the telemetry device radiates is 9 dBm when impedance is perfectly matched. Higher output power could obviously improve the signal strength and distance range measurement. The main constraint which limits increasing radiation power is the specific absorption rate (SAR). We developed a COMSOL stimulation to test our prototype's SAR simulation, which is based on COMSOL application 2190 [25], [26]. The brain's geometry was built based on "The Stanford volume data archive" [27], and Schmid et al. 's work on dielectric properties of human brain tissue [28]. The antenna was placed 1 mm away from the left side of the head. Table III lists parameters.

Fig. 17 demonstrates that spatial peak SAR is less than 0.03 W/kg as averaged over 1 g of tissue, which is far below the IEEE regulations [29]: maximal 8 W/kg as averaged over 1 g of tissue and 0.4 W/kg as averaged over the whole body. We tried to increase the current and found that when the radiation power increased to 30 dBm (current is 0.14 A), spatial peak SAR reached the IEEE limit. We predict that this maximal radiation power could be 141 times higher SNR at the same measurement distance, or a 5.2 times further measurement distance (coupling factor deceases as the distance 1/R³).

C. Analog-to-Digital Convertor

Currently the maximal ADC input from the half wave rectifier is 1 V (when impedance matched), which doesn't make full use of the ADC dynamic range (3.3 V). In the future, we could amplify the signals from the half wave rectifier by a factor of 3 before sending them to the ADC.

In addition, currently we adapt the 12 bits ADC. In the future, a standalone ADC which has higher resolution could improve the signal quality performance.

This noise is due to the half wave rectifier can't "catch" this slow input signal. 3) other noise from the environmental interference [30]. However the signal from the moving average was not satisfactory. A further signal processing method is based on the moving average, known as Local Regression [31]. This algorithm assigned regression weights to the value within a dynamic range with a length chosen to be 5:

\[w_i = \left(1 - \left| \frac{x - x_i}{d(x)} \right|^3 \right)^3 \tag{10}\]

where x is the predicted value associated with the original signal data to be smoothed, \(x_i\) is the neighbor of x in the dynamic range, and d(x) is half of the dynamic range length at the x-axis. Based on these weights, we performed a weighted linear least-squares regression. The regression used a second degree polynomial. Fig. 18 shows the final denoised result.

VI. CONCLUSION

This paper proposes a wearable read out system for a passive resonance pressure sensor to be used for wireless continuous ICP monitoring. The reader can work over a wide frequency range

(35 MHz to 2.7 GHz), which gives the reader freedom to design different size/different pattern sensors, for which resonant frequency may vary. This reader can work at high frequency, which can be used with ultra-small size sensors, which always resonate at high frequencies. The fine resolution and low phase noise allow the reader to detect slight pressure changes. The whole design could be packaged into a helmet, with low additional weight (<50 g), which provide automatic and continuous daily ICP monitoring.
